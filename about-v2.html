I'm Federico A. Monroy — a UX Designer & Strategist and behavioral analytics practitioner. My degree is in Industrial Design, which isn't a fun fact — it's how I think. Proyectual reasoning, systems constraints, and human context came before Figma. That foundation still shapes how I instrument a product, read a session replay, and decide what to build next. Comfortable across any tech stack and any AI toolset. The approach stays constant; the tools adapt to what the team already uses.

Over seven years across fintech, enterprise, government, and e-commerce — sometimes leading teams, sometimes embedded solo — I've always worked at the edge of design, research, and product. Not entirely any one of them. All three at once.

I don't think of analytics as a separate discipline from design.
They're the same loop, just at different speeds.

My approach

Most teams don't know where users drop off or which features get ignored. I build the framework to find out — fast. With behavioral analytics, quick research cycles, or both. The output is always concrete: a redesign, an onboarding improvement, an adoption fix, or a ticket with evidence attached. Your team prioritizes. I make sure there's always something to prioritize.

Behavior before pixels
Understanding what users actually do — not what they say — shapes every design decision I make.
Evidence, not assumptions
Every frame I hand off has a reason that traces back to something a real user did, or didn't do.
Systems over screens
I design the framework that produces the screens — not just the screens themselves.
Clean handoff is design
I work inside existing design systems. No new variants without engineering alignment. The dev experience is part of the UX.

What colleagues say

The most consistent feedback I get from product managers and engineers is that I make the invisible visible. I surface patterns they didn't know existed, frame them in a way that's actionable, and show up with something concrete — not just insights, but a direction.

I also ask questions that aren't strictly "UX questions." What does this cost to build? What's the edge case if the user does X? What metric would tell us this worked? I think that's just part of taking the work seriously.

Outside of work

I'm genuinely curious about behavioral psychology — how habits form, how decisions compound, how small system changes produce large behavior shifts. That curiosity isn't separate from my work; it's basically the same problem at a different scale.

I think a lot about personal systems: how to work with more clarity and intention, how to make good defaults automatic. I'm currently working on improving habits around health, focus, and learning. And I'm quietly building things on the side — some are consulting projects, some are experiments.
